Research

Preserving Requirements-Driven Development in the Age of AI

Undergraduate research · ISCAP Conference 2025

Overview

This research project examines how traditional requirements-driven development practices interact with prompt-driven, AI-assisted code generation. I compared workflows where requirements lead and guide implementation versus those where large language models generate code directly from high-level prompts.

Problem

As teams increasingly experiment with AI-assisted development, it is easy for requirements discipline to erode. When code can be generated quickly from prompts, there is a risk that important quality attributes, constraints, and stakeholder needs get lost or only discovered late in the process. I wanted to understand how much this shift actually affects the resulting systems.

Approach

I designed an empirical study that compared multiple AI models and workflows against established requirements engineering standards. For each scenario, I captured both the requirements artifacts and the resulting code, then evaluated them using ISO/IEC 25010 quality attributes and IEEE 830 / ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148 guidance.

Methods & tooling

  • Defined repeatable prompts and requirements sets for comparing workflows.
  • Ran cross-model comparisons across several large language models.
  • Evaluated code quality, architecture, and maintainability using established quality models.
  • Analyzed differences in how requirements were reflected or dropped in AI-generated implementations.

Key findings & tradeoffs

  • Pure prompt-driven workflows can yield impressive prototypes quickly but often under-specify edge cases and non-functional requirements.
  • Combining structured requirements with AI assistance tends to produce more maintainable designs, at the cost of slower initial iteration.
  • Clear traceability from requirement to code remains valuable, even when LLMs accelerate implementation.

What I learned

This work strengthened my belief that AI tools are most effective when anchored by thoughtful requirements and human judgment. It also gave me language and frameworks for talking about software quality beyond just \"does it work,\" which I now bring into my own projects and collaborations.
