Preserving Requirements-Driven Development in the Age of AI

Empirical study comparing structured requirements vs. prompt-only LLM development workflows

  • Empirical Study
  • ISO/IEC 25010
  • IEEE 830
  • LLM Evaluation
  • Requirements Engineering
Conference paper — ISCAP 2025

Overview

This research examined whether structured requirements documentation meaningfully improves the quality of software artifacts produced by large language models. As AI-assisted development becomes the norm, a critical question is emerging: does the discipline of requirements engineering still matter, or can teams skip directly to prompting?

I designed and executed an empirical study comparing two development workflows — one grounded in structured requirements documents (IEEE 830 / ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148 compliant), one relying on prompt-only LLM interaction — and evaluated the resulting artifacts against ISO/IEC 25010 quality attributes. Findings were presented at the ISCAP Conference 2025.

Research Question

As LLMs become capable of generating substantial amounts of code from natural language prompts, practitioners are debating whether formal requirements processes retain value. The risk is real: if teams abandon structured requirements in favor of direct prompting, they may gain short-term speed while eroding architectural coherence, traceability, and long-term maintainability.

The study asked: Do structured requirements documents, when provided to LLMs, produce software artifacts with measurably higher structural integrity, maintainability, and traceability than prompt-only generation?

Research Design

The study used a controlled comparison framework. For each development task:

  1. Condition A: Provide a structured requirements document (IEEE 830-compliant) to the LLM as context before generating code.
  2. Condition B: Provide only a natural language prompt describing the desired behavior, without formal requirements.

The same tasks were run across multiple LLM systems to test consistency across models. Generated artifacts were then evaluated against ISO/IEC 25010 quality sub-characteristics: functional suitability, maintainability, and analyzability. A traceability analysis was also conducted to assess whether generated components could be mapped back to documented requirements.

The evaluation rubric was developed from ISO/IEC 25010 and IEEE 830 standards, providing a principled, reproducible scoring framework rather than subjective code review.

Key Findings

Quality degradation without requirements is measurable and consistent. Across tasks and models, prompt-only generation produced artifacts with lower scores on maintainability and traceability sub-characteristics. The degradation was not marginal — it was structurally significant, manifesting as missing edge case handling, implicit assumptions baked into architecture, and components that could not be cleanly traced to stated functional needs.

LLM consistency varied significantly across models. The study found notable architectural divergence between models on the same requirements-grounded task, raising questions about which models are most appropriate for use in long-horizon development where consistency matters.

Requirements documents constrain beneficial ambiguity resolution. Interestingly, structured requirements also constrained certain creative problem-solving behaviors in LLMs. This tradeoff — predictability vs. creative flexibility — is an area for further investigation.

What I Learned

  • Designing a rigorous empirical study requires specifying evaluation criteria before generating the artifacts being evaluated. Post-hoc rubric construction introduces measurement bias that is difficult to correct.
  • ISO and IEEE standards, often dismissed as academic overhead, provide genuinely useful evaluation vocabulary. ISO/IEC 25010's quality sub-characteristics gave the study a principled, reproducible measurement framework that would have been difficult to construct from scratch.
  • Presenting findings at a conference — building a narrative that is persuasive to a mixed academic/practitioner audience — required distilling complex empirical data into clear, contestable claims. That skill transfers directly to technical communication at work.
